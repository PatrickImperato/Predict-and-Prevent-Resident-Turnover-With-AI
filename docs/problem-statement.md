# Problem Statement

Resident turnover is one of the most expensive operational problems in property management.

Replacing a resident requires multiple costly steps.

These include:

- marketing the vacant unit
- leasing staff time
- preparing the unit for the next resident
- vacancy loss during the turnover period

Even a short vacancy window can significantly reduce property revenue.

## The core challenge

In most properties, signs that a resident is unhappy appear long before they decide to move.

Examples include:

- repeated maintenance issues
- unresolved service complaints
- negative interactions with property staff
- declining engagement with the property

The problem is not that these signals do not exist.

The problem is that they are scattered across different systems and conversations.

Examples include:

- maintenance software
- leasing notes
- staff conversations
- email communication
- internal spreadsheets

Because the information is fragmented, property teams rarely connect the signals early enough.

## What happens today

Most property teams operate in a reactive workflow.

A resident decides not to renew.

Only then does the property team realize something went wrong.

At that point the opportunity to retain the resident is already lost.

## The opportunity

If property teams could identify retention risk earlier, they could respond with targeted interventions.

Examples include:

- service recovery actions
- concierge support
- maintenance prioritization
- retention incentives

The goal is not just to identify churn risk.

The goal is to give property teams a clear operational workflow for acting on that risk.

## Product hypothesis

If property teams are given a system that:

1. surfaces early signals of resident dissatisfaction  
2. organizes resident history and service issues  
3. allows teams to trigger structured interventions  

then they can improve resident satisfaction and reduce preventable turnover.

This project explores what that product could look like.
